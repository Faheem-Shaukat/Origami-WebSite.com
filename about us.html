Inventor(Of Paper)



Cai Lun was a Chinese court official and inventor during the Eastern Han Dynasty, best known for revolutionizing writing and communication by inventing a new method of papermaking around 105 CE.His contribution laid the foundation for modern writing, printing, art, and origami, making him one of the most influential inventors in history Cai Lun is still honored in China today as the "patron of papermaking," and his invention is regarded as one of the Four Great Inventions of ancient China.



Father Of Origami


Akira Yoshizawa was a Japanese origamist, considered to be the grandmaster of origami. He is widely recognized for his work in raising origami from a craft to a living art form. Yoshizawa devised many new folding techniques during his lifetime. According to his own estimation made in 1989, he created more than 50,000 models, of which only a few hundred designs were presented as diagrams in his 18 books. Yoshizawa acted as an international cultural ambassador for Japan throughout his career. About Origami

What Is Origami?

Origami is the Japanese art of paper folding, combining simplicity and precision to transform a flat square of paper into shapes with volume, form, and meaning. The word “origami” comes from ori (folding) and kami (paper). Unlike crafts that use cutting or glue, traditional origami relies purely on folding techniques. Its simplicity masks the complexity and beauty possible within each fold.

A Brief History of Origami

Origami started as a ceremonial craft in East Asia—used in religious rituals, gift-giving, and cultural customs. As paper technology and availability improved, origami moved beyond ritual into everyday life and artistic expression. One of the earliest formal instructions appears in books like Senbazuru Orikata (late 18th century), which popularized models like the paper crane.

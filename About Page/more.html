About Origami

The crane in particular became an international symbol of peace, helped by stories like that of Sadako Sasaki, who folded cranes hoping for healing after Hiroshima. Today, that symbolism is deeply tied to origami's identity.

Origami as Practice and Art

Origami is more than pretty shapes: it’s also a practice in patience, concentration, and problem-solving. Folding paper improves fine motor skills, spatial reasoning, and the ability to follow complex instructions.

Artists and creators push origami’s boundaries with techniques like:

  • Wet-folding: allowing more sculptural, curved forms.
  • Modular origami: combining multiple folded units into one structure.
  • Pureland origami: simplifying fold types for beginners.

Examples:

  • A traditional origami crane — symbolic and relatively simple for beginners.
  • A lotus flower origami — shows more intricate shaping and layered petals.

Origami in the Modern World

Origami’s influence reaches far beyond art. Engineers and designers adapt folding patterns for practical and technological uses:

  • Foldable solar panels for spacecraft.
  • Medical devices like stents that expand via origami-inspired folding.
  • Architectural installations and deployable structures.

Our Purpose

This website exists to share and celebrate all aspects of origami: tradition, technique, creativity, and symbolism. Whether you are:

Symbolism in Origami

Many origami figures carry rich symbolic meaning:

  • Crane (Orizuru): peace, hope, healing — especially associated with the story of Sadako and senbazuru (folding 1,000 cranes).
  • Lotus: purity, enlightenment, rising above hardship.
  • Butterfly: transformation, love, renewal.
  • Frog or Toad: good luck, safe travel in some cultures.

Understanding symbolism adds emotional depth to every fold—each model becomes not just art, but a message or meditation.
